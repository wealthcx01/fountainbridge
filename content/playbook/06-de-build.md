> **Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (Bill Aulet). Our own application of Aulet's method. Steps 20–23.

Everything so far is a well-reasoned hypothesis. This question turns it into evidence — cheaply, before you
over-build. It's also where Foundry's speed advantage is real: because the lanes can build and revise fast
behind the gate, you can run more honest experiments per week than a traditional team, as long as you're
testing the *risky* things and not polishing the safe ones.

## Step 20 — Identify Key Assumptions
**What it means.** Extract the specific beliefs your plan depends on — the ones that, if wrong, sink the
venture — and isolate them from the things you already know.
**For a Foundry venture.** Write them as a list in the repo; each becomes a testable ticket. Rank by
"how fatal if false" × "how unsure am I," and attack the top of that list first.
**Example.** "Physios will introduce us," "patients will do a daily plan for 8 weeks," "guidance measurably
lowers re-injury" — each a load-bearing assumption.
**Trap.** Testing the assumptions that are easy or flattering to test instead of the ones that would hurt
most if false.

## Step 21 — Test Key Assumptions
**What it means.** Design the cheapest experiment that could *disprove* each assumption, and run it. The goal
is truth fast, not confirmation.
**For a Foundry venture.** Lanes stand up landing pages, opt-in tests, concierge prototypes, and fake-door
tests quickly — but any experiment that touches a real person (a send, a sign-up) still passes the approval
gate. Fast to build, still consent-first to release.
**Example.** A single approved opt-in send to one clinic's list: do patients actually click to start? One
real signal beats a month of opinion.
**Trap.** Running experiments that can only succeed. If a test can't come back "no," it isn't a test.

## Step 22 — Define the Minimum Viable Business Product (MVBP)
**What it means.** The smallest thing that (a) delivers real value, (b) the customer *pays* for, and (c)
starts your feedback loop. Note "business" — it must transact, not just demo.
**For a Foundry venture.** Scope the MVBP as a tight set of tickets aimed only at the value prop (Step 8).
Everything not required to deliver and charge for that value is deferred to a follow-up ticket.
**Example.** One guided rehab plan, a paywall after the free week, and a progress signal the physio can see —
nothing else, but a real paid loop.
**Trap.** Confusing an MVP demo with an MVBP. If no money changes hands, you haven't tested the business.

## Step 23 — Show That "The Dogs Will Eat the Dog Food"
**What it means.** Prove real customers actually adopt, use, and keep paying — behaviour, not surveys. The
metaphor is blunt on purpose: it doesn't matter how good the food is if the dogs won't eat it.
**For a Foundry venture.** Instrument the full life-cycle use case (Step 6) and watch the cohort: activation,
week-4 retention, renewal. The studio's activity and health feeds make this real usage visible, not
anecdotal.
**Example.** Of the first cohort, do they finish week 4 and renew? Sustained use and repeat payment is the
proof; praise isn't.
**Trap.** Declaring victory on sign-ups. Acquisition is easy to fake; *retention* is the honest signal.
