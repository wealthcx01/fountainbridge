# The build arc

Adapted from Bill Aulet's *Disciplined Entrepreneurship* (MIT), which frames company-building as 24
steps grouped under six questions. We keep the discipline and re-tell it for a Foundry venture: one
co-founded with Bruntsfield, built by agent lanes, sold on interest, not cold outreach.

The order matters. Most ventures die because they answer question four (how do you make money) before
question one (who is your customer). Resist that.

## 1 — Who is your customer?

You cannot build for everyone, so stop trying. Narrow until it hurts.

- **Segment the market.** List the distinct groups who might want this, described by who they *are*
  and what they're trying to get done — not by demographics alone.
- **Pick one beachhead.** A single segment where the same product, sold the same way, wins the same
  way for everyone in it. Small enough to dominate, large enough to matter, reachable without a
  miracle. You expand *from* a won beachhead; you never start diffuse.
- **Profile the end user, then a persona.** Not "SMEs" — a named, specific person: what a day looks
  like, what they fear, who they answer to, where they already spend money. If you can't write the
  persona from memory, you don't know your customer yet.
- **Size the beachhead honestly.** Bottom-up: number of end users × what each realistically spends.
  A believable small number beats a fantasy TAM.

*Foundry note:* THE RESET's beachhead isn't "people in transition" — it's a specific, reachable slice
of them who have already raised a hand. The manifest's founder identity and the venture's Workspace
domain exist precisely so you can reach that slice lawfully and personally.

## 2 — What can you do for your customer?

Value is not features. It is a measurable change in the customer's world.

- **Map the full life-cycle use case** — how they discover, buy, onboard, use, pay, and get value,
  start to finish. Gaps here are where ventures leak.
- **Spec the product at a high level** — a picture of what it does, not how it's built.
- **Quantify the value proposition** — in the customer's own terms and numbers. "Saves Ross six hours
  a week and one dropped client a month" beats "increases efficiency." If you can't quantify it,
  you're guessing at value.
- **Name the next ten customers** — real people who fit the persona. Not a list you *hope* exists;
  ten you could email today. If you can't, the beachhead is wrong.

## 3 — How does your customer acquire your product?

- **Define your core** — the one thing you do better than anyone that is hard to copy. Everything
  else you can buy, borrow, or defer. (This is where the [moats](moats) chapter starts.)
- **Chart your competitive position** — on the two axes your customer's *top two priorities* define.
  Win on the dimensions they actually care about, not the ones you find interesting.
- **Map the decision-making unit** — the champion, the economic buyer, the end user, and the people
  who can veto. Selling to one when another decides is the quiet killer of good products.
- **Design the acquisition process** — the concrete path from "never heard of you" to "paying." For a
  Foundry venture this runs on **interest, not interruption** (see [Selling](selling)).

## 4 — How do you make money?

- **Choose a business model** that fits how the customer already buys, and how you keep earning as you
  deliver more value.
- **Set a pricing framework**, not a number — anchored to the value you quantified, not your costs.
- **Estimate lifetime value (LTV)** and the **cost to acquire a customer (COCA/CAC).** If it costs
  more to win a customer than they're worth, no amount of growth fixes it — it accelerates the loss.

## 5 — How do you design and build it?

Build the smallest thing that lets a real customer get real value — and that produces evidence you're
right or wrong. In the studio this is a lane: one ticket, one branch, one PR, shipped through a human
gate. The discipline of "smallest thing that proves value" and "one change at a time" is the same at
the product level as it is at the commit level.

## 6 — How do you scale?

Only after the beachhead is won. Scaling a business that hasn't proven value at small scale just
manufactures a bigger version of the same problem. Expand to adjacent segments that share your core,
compound the [moats](moats), and let each won market fund the next.

---

**The test of this chapter:** at any moment you should be able to say which of the six questions
you've genuinely answered with evidence, and which you're still assuming. Assumptions aren't failures
— pretending they're answers is.
