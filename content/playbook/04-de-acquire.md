> **Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (Bill Aulet). Our own application of Aulet's method. Steps 12–14.

Wanting the product and *acquiring* it are different things. This question maps the machinery of a
purchase — who's involved, what the steps really are, and how big the opportunity gets if the beachhead
works. For a consent-first venture, this is also where "how they acquire it" and "how we're allowed to
reach them" have to line up.

## Step 12 — Determine the Decision-Making Unit (DMU)
**What it means.** Identify everyone involved in a purchase: the end user, the economic buyer (controls
the money), the champion, and any influencers, vetoes, or gatekeepers.
**For a Foundry venture.** Often the user and the payer differ — and a third party (a clinic, an employer)
is the trusted introducer. Interest-based GTM lives or dies on that introducer's consent, so name them
explicitly.
**Example.** User = Priya; champion = her physio; economic buyer = Priya (or her insurer); gatekeeper =
the clinic's data/privacy policy.
**Trap.** Selling only to the enthusiastic user while ignoring the buyer or the veto who quietly kills it.

## Step 13 — Map the Process to Acquire a Paying Customer
**What it means.** Trace every step from first awareness to paid, cradle to grave — including budget
authority, sales cycle length, and regulatory or approval hurdles.
**For a Foundry venture.** Every outbound touch in this map is an **external action** that stops at the
approval gate before it fires. Mapping the process *is* mapping where human consent is required — design
them as one thing, not two.
**Example.** Physio opt-in → welcome send → free week → conversion send → checkout → clinic-visible
progress. Each send is drafted by a lane and released only on approval.
**Trap.** Assuming a short, clean funnel when the real process has a gatekeeper, a compliance check, and a
three-week gap you didn't budget for.

## Step 14 — Calculate the TAM for Follow-on Markets
**What it means.** Estimate the *additional* markets you could enter after winning the beachhead — adjacent
segments or new offerings to the same customers. This is the bridge to the scaling question.
**For a Foundry venture.** Sketch it to show the beachhead is a doorway, not a dead end — but do **not**
let follow-on markets pull focus. They exist to prove the upside and sequence later work, nothing more yet.
**Example.** From post-op recovery → chronic-pain management → clinic-side analytics → elite-sport
recovery: same Core (the outcomes dataset), widening markets.
**Trap.** Working the follow-on markets *now*. They're a map for later; the beachhead is the only battle
you're actually fighting.
