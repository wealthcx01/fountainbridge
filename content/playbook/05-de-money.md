> **Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (Bill Aulet). Our own application of Aulet's method. Steps 15–19.

A product that helps people but can't capture value is a hobby. This question builds the economic engine:
how you charge, what a customer is worth, what it costs to win one, and whether the two numbers point the
right way. The last comparison — value in vs. cost to acquire — is the one that decides if the venture is
a business.

## Step 15 — Design a Business Model
**What it means.** Choose *how* you capture value — subscription, usage, licensing, transaction, freemium —
matched to how the customer gets and perceives value. The model is a strategic choice, not a price.
**For a Foundry venture.** Prefer models that compound with the interest-based, consent-first motion:
recurring value → recurring revenue, with the introducer's trust intact.
**Example.** A monthly subscription for the guided plan, with a clinic-referral arrangement — recurring,
and aligned with the champion who introduced it.
**Trap.** Defaulting to "we'll charge a subscription" without asking whether the customer experiences value
continuously or just once.

## Step 16 — Set Your Pricing Framework
**What it means.** Price against the *quantified value* (Step 8) and the customer's alternatives — capturing
a fair fraction of value created, not cost-plus. Consider early-customer, and champion economics.
**For a Foundry venture.** Anchor price to the value number you can defend, and keep it legible; opaque
pricing corrodes the trust that consent-based GTM runs on.
**Example.** Priced as a fraction of "two weeks faster, lower re-injury risk," well under the cost of a
re-tear — a number the persona accepts without a spreadsheet.
**Trap.** Cost-plus pricing, which ignores value entirely and usually leaves most of it on the table.

## Step 17 — Calculate Lifetime Value (LTV)
**What it means.** The total profit from an average customer over the relationship — from revenue, margin,
retention, and expansion, discounted for time.
**For a Foundry venture.** LTV is the ceiling on what you can spend to acquire a customer. Retention is the
biggest lever, so the "boring middle" (onboarding, habit) is an economic decision, not just UX.
**Example.** Subscription margin × expected months retained + any referral/expansion value → a defensible
per-customer LTV.
**Trap.** Inflating LTV with a fantasy retention curve. Use evidence; a hopeful churn rate hides a broken
model.

## Step 18 — Map the Sales Process
**What it means.** Design how you'll actually sell across the venture's life — the short-term (founder-led,
high-touch), medium-term, and long-term (self-serve, channel) motions — and the cost of each.
**For a Foundry venture.** Early on the sales process is largely *approved sends and honest conversations*;
later it's more automated. Each stage still ends at the same gate — nothing ships to a human without a yes.
**Example.** Phase 1: founder + physio champions, hand-approved sends. Phase 2: templated opt-in journeys.
Phase 3: clinic self-serve onboarding.
**Trap.** Assuming the cheap, scalable channel works from day one. Early sales are expensive and manual on
purpose — that's how you learn.

## Step 19 — Calculate the Cost of Customer Acquisition (COCA)
**What it means.** The fully-loaded cost to acquire one customer over time — all sales and marketing spend
divided by customers won — and crucially, how it *falls* as the motion matures.
**For a Foundry venture.** Interest-based acquisition should drive COCA down structurally: reaching people
who already want the thing is cheaper than interrupting people who don't. Prove that, and the engine works.
**Example.** High COCA in the founder-led phase, dropping sharply as champion referrals and opt-in journeys
compound.
**Trap.** The venture-killer: **COCA ≥ LTV**. If it costs more to win a customer than they're worth, no
amount of growth saves you — fix the model before you scale it.
