> **7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy** (Hamilton Helmer). Our own explanation and
> application of Helmer's framework — not a reproduction of the book. Deep companion to *Moats — the
> seven powers*.

Helmer's definition is exacting: **Power** is a condition that creates *sustained differential returns* —
the ability to earn more than your cost of capital, and to keep it, because competitors can't arbitrage the
advantage away. Every Power has two inseparable parts:

- **Benefit** — it improves cash flow: higher prices, lower costs, or less capital required.
- **Barrier** — the reason a competitor *can't or won't* copy the benefit away. The benefit is the easy
  half; the **barrier is the strategy.** A benefit without a barrier is just a nice quarter.

Helmer also insists Power is **dynamic**: each Power can only be *established* during a particular window of
a business's life — Origination, Takeoff, or Stability. Miss the window and the door often closes. For a
Foundry venture, that turns strategy into *timing*: name the Power you're building (it starts as the Core
from DE Step 10), and build it while it's still buildable.

## 1. Scale Economies
**Benefit.** Unit costs fall as volume rises — you can profitably price where a smaller rival can't.
**Barrier.** A challenger would have to match your scale to match your costs, and the cost of buying that
share is prohibitive.
**When buildable.** *Takeoff* — as volume compounds during rapid growth.
**How a venture earns it.** Concentrate on the beachhead until you're the volume leader in it; design fixed
costs (content, model, infrastructure) that spread across a growing base rather than recur per customer.

## 2. Network Economies
**Benefit.** The product becomes more valuable to each user as the installed base grows.
**Barrier.** A new entrant is stuck at low value with few users while you compound — the gap widens, not
narrows.
**When buildable.** *Takeoff* — network value accelerates with the user base.
**How a venture earns it.** Build in a reason users benefit from each other (shared data, matching,
community). Interest-based GTM helps: each satisfied cohort recruits the next, feeding the network honestly.

## 3. Counter-Positioning
**Benefit.** You adopt a superior business model the incumbent can't follow without damaging its existing
business.
**Barrier.** *Collateral damage* — copying you would cannibalise the incumbent's profits, so its own board
rationally refuses. Its strength becomes its cage.
**When buildable.** *Origination* — it's the newcomer's founding move, chosen at the start.
**How a venture earns it.** Design the model so success *hurts* the incumbent to imitate — a pricing,
distribution, or consent-first motion that their legacy economics won't let them match. This is often a
small venture's single best weapon against a large one.

## 4. Switching Costs
**Benefit.** Customers pay more to stay than a rival would have to charge to lure them, because leaving is
costly.
**Barrier.** To win your customer, a competitor must compensate for that switching cost on top of matching
you — often uneconomic.
**When buildable.** *Takeoff into Stability* — it accrues as customers embed data, habits, and integrations.
**How a venture earns it.** Let customers invest in the product over time (their history, configuration,
outcomes) so it grows more theirs — earned through delivered value, never hostage-taking.

## 5. Branding
**Benefit.** A higher willingness to pay for an objectively identical offering, because your name carries
trust or affect.
**Barrier.** Reputation is built only over years of consistent delivery — a competitor can't buy the history.
**When buildable.** *Origination through Stability* — begun at founding, real only after sustained years.
**How a venture earns it.** Be relentlessly consistent on the promise the persona cares about; for a
consent-first venture, brand *is* the accumulated trust of every honest, approved interaction. One bad send
spends years of it.

## 6. Cornered Resource
**Benefit.** Preferential access to a coveted resource — data, talent, a license, a supply — on terms others
can't get.
**Barrier.** Exclusive control: a patent, an exclusive agreement, or a genuinely scarce asset others simply
can't obtain.
**When buildable.** *Origination* — usually secured at or near founding.
**How a venture earns it.** Lock in the scarce thing early. For a Foundry venture the Cornered Resource is
often a **proprietary dataset or partnership** (the DE Step 10 Core) secured before competitors know the
market exists.

## 7. Process Power
**Benefit.** Lower cost and/or better product from organisational processes competitors can't replicate.
**Barrier.** *Hysteresis* — the processes are complex, tacit, and improved over so long that copying them
takes years even if fully visible.
**When buildable.** *Stability* — it emerges only through sustained operational refinement.
**How a venture earns it.** This is the long game, and where Foundry's substrate helps: the lanes, gstack's
plan-review-ship discipline, and gbrain's accumulating memory *are* a process that compounds — each venture
inherits a maturing operating machine rather than reinventing one.

## Using this
You don't need all seven — you need **one**, deliberately built while its window is open, defending a
business you've already proven with Disciplined Entrepreneurship. Name your intended Power early, and let it
tell the lanes what to deepen and protect. Value you can't defend is a treadmill; a Power is how a Foundry
venture steps off it.
