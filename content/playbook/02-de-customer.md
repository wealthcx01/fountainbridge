> **Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (Bill Aulet, *24 Steps to a Successful Startup*). Our own
> explanation and application of Aulet's method — not a reproduction of the book. Steps 1–6.

The first question decides everything downstream. A Foundry venture doesn't get to "everyone with a
problem" — it earns one specific group of people who feel one specific pain, and grows out from there.
Because our go-to-market is **interest-based** (no cold outreach; we reach people who've signalled they
want this), a sharp customer definition isn't optional — it's what makes a consent-first launch even
possible. You can't attract interest from a market you haven't named.

## Step 1 — Market Segmentation
**What it means.** Brainstorm widely, then break the world into distinct market opportunities — groups
who buy similar things for similar reasons. Breadth first, so you don't fall in love with the first idea.
**For a Foundry venture.** Run this in a lane as a research pass: enumerate 6–12 candidate segments,
each with the end user, the task they're trying to get done, and why now.
**Example.** A "reset" wellbeing product might segment into new parents, shift workers, post-injury
recovery, corporate burnout programs, and elite-sport recovery — each a different buyer and rhythm.
**Trap.** Segmenting by demographics ("25–34, urban") instead of by *job-to-be-done*. Age doesn't buy;
a shared, urgent task does.

## Step 2 — Select a Beachhead Market
**What it means.** Choose **one** segment to win completely before expanding. Small enough to dominate,
real enough to matter.
**For a Foundry venture.** Pick the beachhead where interest is easiest to earn honestly — an existing
community, list, or channel where these people already gather and would welcome the thing.
**Example.** Of the resets, "post-injury recovery, routed through physiotherapy clinics" — a bounded,
reachable group with an acute need and a natural referrer.
**Trap.** Picking a beachhead because it's *big*. A beachhead's job is to be *winnable*; scale comes later.

## Step 3 — Build an End User Profile
**What it means.** Describe the specific person who will actually *use* the product (distinct from who
pays). A tight, recognisable profile — not a demographic range.
**For a Foundry venture.** Write it down in the venture repo; the lanes and the studio reference it so
every later decision (copy, features, sends) points at the same person.
**Example.** "Recovering from an ACL repair, 6–16 weeks post-op, doing daily home rehab, anxious about
re-injury, follows their physio's plan on their phone."
**Trap.** A profile so broad that two people who fit it would want opposite things.

## Step 4 — Calculate the TAM for the Beachhead
**What it means.** Size the beachhead's Total Addressable Market — annual revenue if you won 100% of it.
Bottom-up (number of end users × annual value), not a top-down fraction of a huge number.
**For a Foundry venture.** Aim for a beachhead TAM that's meaningful but conquerable (Aulet's rule of
thumb: roughly \$20M–\$100M/year). Big enough to build a real business, small enough to own.
**Example.** ~200k relevant post-op patients/year × plausible annual value → a bottom-up figure you can
defend line by line.
**Trap.** "1% of a \$10B market." Top-down percentages are wishes, not markets.

## Step 5 — Profile the Persona
**What it means.** Turn the end-user profile into **one real, named person** — the archetypal buyer for
the beachhead — with their priorities, watering holes, and buying criteria.
**For a Foundry venture.** The persona is the audience for every interest-based send. If you can't name
where they already pay attention, you can't reach them on consent.
**Example.** "Priya, 34, three weeks post-op, wants to trust she's rehabbing correctly; reads her clinic's
handouts, in two recovery Facebook groups." Every send is written *to* Priya.
**Trap.** Inventing a persona instead of grounding it in primary conversations with real people.

## Step 6 — Full Life-Cycle Use Case
**What it means.** Map how the persona discovers, evaluates, buys, onboards, uses, pays for, and gets
value from the product — end to end, before you build.
**For a Foundry venture.** This map exposes every point where an *external* action fires (a send, a
sign-up, a payment) — exactly the points that pass through the approval gate. Design the journey and the
gates together.
**Example.** Physio mentions it → Priya sees an opt-in send → free first week → guided plan → subscribes
→ shares in her group. Each arrow is a step you can instrument.
**Trap.** Designing acquisition and ignoring the boring middle — onboarding and habit — where ventures
actually die.
