# Moats — the seven powers

Adapted from Hamilton Helmer's *7 Powers*. Helmer's insight is simple and unforgiving: **strategy is
the study of what makes a business *durably* valuable.** Not "valuable today" — durable. A power is
present only when it has two halves at once:

- a **Benefit** — it lets you charge more, or cost less, or both; and
- a **Barrier** — a reason your competitors *can't or won't* copy it, even though they can see it.

A benefit without a barrier is a nice quarter, then a price war. Foundry ventures are small and
co-created, so we can't out-spend anyone. We win by building barriers early and on purpose. Here are
the seven, with where in a venture's life you can actually build each.

## The seven

1. **Scale economies** — your cost per unit falls as you get bigger, faster than a rival can match.
   *Buildable late,* once you have volume. Rarely a startup's first power; don't kid yourself here early.

2. **Network economies** — the product gets more valuable to each user as more users join. *Build the
   conditions early* (make sharing and connection native), harvest late. The barrier is brutal for
   latecomers: they'd have to move the whole network at once.

3. **Counter-positioning** — you adopt a new model the incumbent *can't* copy without damaging their
   existing business. *A startup's best early power.* Bruntsfield's co-creation model — agents doing
   the operational work a traditional agency bills by the hour — is counter-positioning: the incumbent
   can't match the price without cannibalising their own economics.

4. **Switching costs** — once a customer is in, leaving is expensive (data, integration, retraining,
   risk). *Build from the first version* by embedding into the customer's real workflow, not bolting
   on beside it.

5. **Branding** — the customer pays more, or trusts faster, because of what your name means. *Compounds
   slowly, from day one.* Every honest interaction is a deposit; every over-claim is a withdrawal.
   This is why the studio's rule is *fail loud, surface everything* — trust is a moat and it's fragile.

6. **Cornered resource** — you have preferential access to something rivals don't: a person, a dataset,
   a relationship, a right. *Foundry's is structural* — the founder is a genuine domain insider
   co-building with us, and the ventures share a substrate (gbrain memory, the contracts, the studio)
   no single founder could build alone.

7. **Process power** — your organisation does something complex better than others can, and it can't
   be bought or copied quickly because it's embedded in how you work. *The studio itself is process
   power in the making:* one-ticket-one-branch, adversarial review, the approval matrix — a way of
   shipping that a competitor would have to rebuild their whole culture to match.

## How to use this

At each stage of the [build arc](build-arc), ask: **which power am I building toward, and does this
next move deposit into it or spend it?** A venture with no answer is running on the treadmill —
growing revenue and defending nothing. Pick your power early (usually counter-positioning or switching
costs for a young venture), and let every other chapter serve it.

> The moat is not a chapter you do once. It's the lens you hold over every other decision.
