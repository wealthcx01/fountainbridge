> **Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (Bill Aulet). Our own application of Aulet's method. Steps 7–11.

Now that you know exactly who the customer is, define what you'll do for them — concretely enough to
build, and valuable enough that they'll change their behaviour to get it. This is where a vague "app for
wellbeing" becomes a specific promise with a number attached.

## Step 7 — High-Level Product Specification
**What it means.** A visual, benefit-led description of what the product does for the persona — brochure,
not blueprint. It commits you to an offering without over-committing to implementation.
**For a Foundry venture.** Draft it in the repo as the shared reference the lanes build against; it turns
into tickets. Keep it benefit-first ("know you're rehabbing correctly"), not feature-first.
**Example.** A guided daily rehab plan, form-check reminders, and a "am I on track?" signal — shown as
three screens, not a database schema.
**Trap.** Speccing features you find clever instead of the outcome the persona is hiring you for.

## Step 8 — Quantify the Value Proposition
**What it means.** Express the benefit in the persona's own terms, as a number, against their status quo:
what improves, by how much.
**For a Foundry venture.** This single quantified claim becomes the spine of all interest-based copy —
honest, specific, measurable. It's also what you'll later prove.
**Example.** "Cut re-injury risk and finish rehab two weeks sooner" — tied to the persona's top priority
from Step 5, not a generic "feel better."
**Trap.** Value stated as *features* ("daily reminders") rather than *gain the customer feels* ("don't
re-tear it").

## Step 9 — Identify Your Next 10 Customers
**What it means.** Name ten real prospects who fit the persona and would plausibly buy — a reality check
that the beachhead isn't a party of one.
**For a Foundry venture.** These ten are your first interest-based conversations. If you can't list ten
reachable, consenting people, the beachhead (Step 2) is wrong — go back.
**Example.** Ten named post-op patients (via two partner clinics) who've agreed to talk — not "people like
Priya" in the abstract.
**Trap.** Listing friends and well-wishers who'll be nice to you instead of buyers who'll be honest.

## Step 10 — Define Your Core
**What it means.** Name the *one* thing you'll do better than anyone that's hard to copy — your source of
enduring advantage. Not a feature; the root of why you'll keep winning.
**For a Foundry venture.** The Core is the seed of a **Power** (see *Moats — the seven powers*). Choosing
it early tells the lanes what to protect and deepen versus what to treat as commodity.
**Example.** A proprietary recovery-outcome dataset from clinic partners that makes the guidance
measurably better the more patients use it — a Core competitors can't buy.
**Trap.** Claiming your brand or your team as the Core on day one, before either is actually rare.

## Step 11 — Chart Your Competitive Position
**What it means.** Plot yourself against alternatives on the **two axes the persona cares most about** (from
Step 5's priorities). Win the top-right; concede the rest.
**For a Foundry venture.** If your Core is right, you should dominate on the persona's two priorities. If
you can't, the value prop or the beachhead needs rework — better to learn it here than after launch.
**Example.** Axes "trustworthy/clinically-grounded" × "effortless daily use" — a quadrant incumbents
(generic fitness apps; paper handouts) leave empty.
**Trap.** Choosing axes where *you* look good instead of the axes the customer actually buys on.
